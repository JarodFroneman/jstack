# Code of Conduct

## Our Standard

JStack is built around rigorous engineering, honest evidence, and respectful
professional disagreement. Contributors, maintainers, and users are expected
to make participation safe, constructive, and useful for people of every
background and level of experience.

Positive behavior includes:

- discussing ideas and evidence without attacking people;
- giving specific, actionable, and technically grounded feedback;
- acknowledging uncertainty, mistakes, and the limits of available evidence;
- respecting privacy, confidentiality, attribution, and intellectual property;
- helping others understand the reasoning behind a decision;
- accepting a maintainer's final scope and safety decision professionally.

Unacceptable behavior includes:

- harassment, threats, intimidation, stalking, or discriminatory conduct;
- sexualized language, imagery, or unwanted attention;
- publishing private information without explicit permission;
- deliberately misleading evidence, benchmarks, security claims, or release
  results;
- disrupting project spaces or repeatedly ignoring maintainer direction;
- using project channels to facilitate malware, credential theft, unauthorized
  access, or other harmful activity.

## Scope

This standard applies in the repository, issue tracker, pull requests,
discussions, release channels, and project-related public or private
interactions where someone is representing JStack.

## Enforcement

Maintainers may edit or remove contributions, comments, or other material that
violates this standard. Responses can include a private warning, temporary
participation restriction, permanent removal, or escalation to the relevant
platform or authority when required.

Report conduct concerns privately to the maintainer through a private contact
channel listed on the maintainer's GitHub profile. Do not publish sensitive
personal details in an issue. Security vulnerabilities must follow
[SECURITY.md](SECURITY.md).

Reports will be reviewed as promptly and impartially as practical. Information
will be shared only with people needed to investigate and resolve the matter.

## Good-Faith Technical Disagreement

Firm criticism of code, architecture, evidence, or project decisions is
welcome. The line is simple: challenge the work with evidence and respect the
person doing it.
